# Design Agent 4: iOS / Apple-Style Designer

## Thesis

Apple-grade restraint is the missing posture, not a missing component. bird-maps.com over-decorates the chrome (138.5px of three-row filter bar, a fixed "3,842 sightings" subhead permanently consuming attention, an auto-expanded legend covering 44.8% of the map) while *under-using* its actual assets — the eBird data is the headline, the iNat photo is the most engaging single pixel surface in the app, and `<dialog>` + the `tablist` contract are battle-tested overlay/navigation primitives the rest of the UI hasn't earned the right to ignore. An iOS-trained designer would do four things in sequence: (1) let content displace chrome rather than coexist with it (chrome shrinks as you scroll, vanishes during interaction, returns when you stop); (2) treat color as scarce — one accent, deployed only at decision points, absent everywhere else; (3) move the photo up to the role of anchor, not asset; (4) accept that San Francisco / system-default IS the brand, and the absence of a webfont is a feature that costs nothing and reads as confident. The entire redesign should feel less like "a website was decorated" and more like "the data was framed."

## Ideas

### 1. Collapsing large title (Apple Notes / Mail / Photos)
The persistent "3,842 sightings, 274 species" subhead is iOS's large-title pattern done badly — it's static. Real large titles render at ~34pt at the top of scroll and collapse into a 17pt inline title in the nav bar as you scroll past the first ~50px. On `feed`/`species` surfaces: "Recent Arizona sightings" renders at 28–34px above the list; on scroll it interpolates into a compact 15px title in the chrome strip while the subhead ("3,842 · updated 11 min ago") fades into a single-line meta. The Sky Atlas mockup's `.sa-context-title` is the *uncollapsed* state — what's missing is the scroll-driven transition.
**Native reference:** Apple Notes title bar; UIKit's `UINavigationBar.prefersLargeTitles = true`.
**What it improves:** Reclaims ~60–80px of permanent vertical chrome on scrolled feed/species lists without removing context. Subhead is large where it has room, vanishes where it doesn't.
**Risk:** Scroll-tied animation requires `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` to switch to a step transition (not a fade). The codebase has zero motion CSS today (Theme 4.5) — this is the first.

### 2. Bottom sheet for species detail with snap points (Apple Maps "Look up")
Replace both the current full-surface detail view AND the proposed Pattern B modal with a bottom sheet that has three snap points: **peek** (~120px — photo thumbnail, common name, one-line "26 sightings this month"), **half** (~50% viewport — adds phenology + family + family color swatch), **full** (~90% — full Wikipedia prose, scrollable). Drag handle, momentum-based snapping, drag-down-past-peek dismisses. The map underneath remains live and pannable in peek/half states — the user can keep their place. Cold-load a shared link at `half` snap (Q5 resolved: there's always a map behind it).
**Native reference:** Apple Maps' POI sheet; `UISheetPresentationController` with `.medium()`/`.large()` detents.
**What it improves:** Removes the "no back button" failure (Finding 2.4) — drag-down dismisses, ESC dismisses, browser back dismisses (with `pushState` per Recommendation 2). Photo-as-anchor is automatic. The map keeps spatial context; users don't lose where they were on tap.
**Risk:** Drag gesture on the sheet conflicts with MapLibre pan if not isolated to the handle + sheet header. Implement with `touch-action: none` on the handle, `touch-action: pan-y` on the sheet content. Native `<dialog>` doesn't have detents — needs a custom focus-trap (or `popover` API + `inert` on background, polyfilled).

### 3. Segmented control for time window (replaces native `<select>`)
The "Time window" `<select>` opens an iOS picker on mobile — a full-screen wheel — for what is fundamentally a 4-option choice (1d / 7d / 14d / 30d). A segmented control renders all four inline at ~32px tall, single-tap, no overlay, no native picker chrome. On desktop this is a 280px-wide pill with 4 segments; on mobile, full-width across the filter sheet's first row.
**Native reference:** `UISegmentedControl`; SwiftUI `Picker(.segmented)`; the iOS Calendar app's Day/Week/Month/Year switcher.
**What it improves:** Three-row mobile FiltersBar collapses by one row immediately. Removes the native picker overlay (which on iOS Safari steals focus and triggers a system animation). Discoverability: all four options are visible without interaction.
**Risk:** Custom segmented controls have a 20-year history of breaking keyboard navigation. Implement as `role="radiogroup"` with arrow-key handling that mirrors the existing `SurfaceNav.tsx:79–108` tablist pattern — there's already a working contract in the codebase.

### 4. Single-accent discipline: sunrise orange (day) / aurora cyan (night), absent everywhere else
The Sky Atlas palette already lands here (`#f5853b` light / `#6db8d4` dark). The discipline iOS designers bring is **subtractive**: accent appears on (a) the active SurfaceNav tab indicator, (b) the active filter chip, (c) the "Open species detail →" CTA, (d) the phenology bars in active months, (e) focus-visible outlines. **Nowhere else.** No accent on family colors (those stay earth-tone, semantic), no accent on counts, no accent on timestamps, no accent decorative gradient on hero text. The Sky Atlas mockup violates this — `.sa-context-meta` "RECENT · LAST 14 DAYS" is in accent for no functional reason, just to add color.
**Native reference:** Apple Photos uses tint blue *only* on selectable controls; the entire Photos app's chrome is greyscale until you can act.
**What it improves:** Color carries meaning — when accent appears, something is interactive. Today bird-maps.com's family colors compete with notable-orange compete with link-blue and nothing reads as "this is the next action."
**Risk:** Voice register decision (Position B) may want accent for the value prop ("updated in real time"). Discipline says: that's prose, prose is colorless, the *freshness dot* next to it carries the accent.

### 5. Cluster bubbles as pills, not solid circles (Apple Maps idiom)
Apple Maps doesn't render clusters as filled circles — it uses minimal pill-shaped capsules with the count in the center, a thin colored stroke, and a subtle fill at low opacity. At small zoom, single observations are 8px dots; at cluster zoom, capsules of 32–48px height with rounded ends. The current cluster bubbles (`#51bbd6` / `#f1f075` / `#f28cb1` solid fills) read as game UI; pills read as cartography. Family-color encoding survives — it goes on the stroke + a 12% fill, with `--color-text-strong` on the count for AA contrast.
**Native reference:** Apple Maps cluster bubbles (since iOS 13); MapKit's `MKMarkerAnnotationView` clustering.
**What it improves:** Map reads as a map, not a chart. Family colors are *legible* at small sizes (a 1.5px stroke is more visually present than 32px of pale fill at distance). Contrast becomes arithmetically enforceable (Finding 5.2) because text always sits on `var(--color-bg-surface)`, not on family-tinted fills that vary across 7+ palette entries.
**Risk:** MapLibre's `circle-stroke-width`/`circle-stroke-color` paint props support this without a custom layer, but `circle-stroke-opacity` is the lever — needs runtime testing against the dark basemap (G7).

### 6. Photo-as-anchor on detail, with overlay text the way Apple Photos handles location
On detail open, the iNat photo loads as the *first paint*, full-bleed across the sheet/surface width, with the species common name overlaid in the lower-left in white at ~36px (extra-bold), scientific name in italic 14px directly below at 85% opacity. A subtle bottom-up `linear-gradient(transparent, rgba(0,0,0,0.7))` makes the text readable without a card. Photo credit ("Mat Hyde · CC BY-NC") sits in the *lower-right* in 10px white at 70% opacity — Apple's exact pattern for embedded photographer credit. The Sky Atlas mockup already drafts this; the implementation note is that the photo must come first in the DOM and be `loading="eager"` (Finding 5.3 notes the `loading="lazy"` gap — for the *anchor* photo, lazy is wrong).
**Native reference:** Apple Photos' photo detail; the Weather app's location hero.
**What it improves:** First paint is the most engaging asset, not a phenology chart of mostly empty bars. CLS is solved by the existing `aspect-ratio: 4/3` mitigation (Finding 5.3).
**Risk:** Species without iNat photos (Phylopic-only fallback) need a deliberate alternative anchor — the family silhouette at 200pt+ on a single-color background tinted by the family color. Not a fallback rectangle of cream.

### 7. System skeletons, not shimmer (and the loading-state visual language overall)
Shimmer animations are cargo-culted from Facebook ca. 2017 and feel busy at iOS scale. Apple's loading pattern is: (a) **content placeholders** are flat grey rectangles at the exact dimensions of incoming content (no animation, no gradient sweep), (b) **progress is implicit** via the system's own deference (the navigation bar shows a thin 2px progress line in tint color, like Safari's URL bar load indicator), (c) **first content wins** — the first paint that's real content displaces all placeholders simultaneously, not row-by-row. For bird-maps.com: the 730px map skeleton (Finding 2.2) becomes a flat `var(--color-bg-tint)` rectangle at the precise map dimensions with a 2px sunrise-orange progress bar at the top of the FiltersBar; the feed shows 12 grey placeholder rows at exact feed-row dimensions; the detail surface shows a grey 4:3 rectangle where the photo will land plus a single grey horizontal line where the title will land.
**Native reference:** SwiftUI `.redacted(reason: .placeholder)`; Safari's URL-bar progress line.
**What it improves:** The cream-on-cream "Loading map…" text becomes a structurally honest skeleton. Loading and empty become visually distinct (empty stays cream + muted text + an actionable hint; loading is grey rectangles + the progress line). Zero motion-CSS until you opt into the progress line, and that line is `<progress>` with native styling.
**Risk:** The progress line needs a real signal — `useBirdData`'s `loading: boolean` is binary, not progressive. Either accept a fake-but-honest "indeterminate" `<progress>` (which iOS itself uses for non-progressive loads) or drop the progress line and rely on placeholder dimensions alone. Indeterminate is fine; it's the "I am working" channel, not the "I am 73% done" channel.

## One bold direction (optional)

**Make the system font the brand.** Don't add a webfont. Don't add a logo wordmark in a custom typeface. The brand name "Bird Maps · Arizona" renders in San Francisco (Apple), Segoe UI Variable (Windows), Roboto (Android) — and that's correct. Each platform's user is reading the wordmark in the typeface they read everything else in, which is the most native a web product can possibly feel. The brand mark (the 26px square with a clipped diamond in the Sky Atlas mockup) carries the visual identity; the wordmark carries semantics. This costs zero perf budget, zero `<link rel="preconnect">`, zero CLS risk from font-load FOUT, and reads as confident in the way only "we don't need a custom typeface to mean something" reads. The competitors (eBird, Merlin, iNaturalist) all reach for branded display fonts and look noisier for it. Sky Atlas could be the bird site that doesn't.

The corollary: be confident with `font-weight: 800` for headlines and `font-weight: 400` for body, `letter-spacing: -0.5px` on display sizes, `font-feature-settings: "tnum"` for tabular numerics on counts/timestamps. SF Pro / Segoe UI Variable / Roboto all support these contracts identically. The 35+ font-size literals (Finding 4.2) collapse into a 6-step modular scale: **11 / 13 / 15 / 17 / 22 / 34** (Apple's HIG defaults adapted for web). That's the actual type system; everything else is variants.
